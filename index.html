This is every Division I men's team this season, placed by how much they shoot the league's two most efficient shots (rim attempts and three-pointers) and how well they shoot them. Color is overall team strength: green is elite, red is not. Notice the green clusters near the top. Let's find out why.

Start with the obvious winners. Michigan, Duke, and Florida sit in the top-right: they shoot a lot of good shots and make them. It would be easy to conclude that the recipe is simply "shoot more of the right shots." Hold that thought.

If volume were the secret, the entire right half of the chart—every high-volume team—should be winning. So look at all of them at once. Some are green. But a lot of them are orange and red. Volume alone is clearly not sorting the good teams from the bad.

Here is the problem with the volume theory. Cal Poly, Elon, and Youngstown State shoot rim-and-threes as often as almost anyone in the country. They are among the highest-volume teams here. And they sit at NET #219, #222, and #227. Shooting more did not save them, because they shoot it badly.

Now the opposite case. Houston, Arizona, Purdue, and Michigan State are below the median in volume. They don't crank up shots. Yet they sit at NET #6, #3, #8, and #11—genuinely elite. They win not by shooting more, but by only taking shots they make.

And this is the punchline. Dim the entire bottom half—every low-quality team—and watch what disappears: all of the green. Across all 365 teams, every single top-25 team is above-median in shot quality. Only 15 of those 25 are above-median in volume. Quality is the price of admission. Volume is optional.

So the answer to our question: the best offenses don't shoot more—they shoot better. Now the chart is yours. To cut through the crowding, we've highlighted the top 10 and bottom 10 teams by default—but explore however you like. The one thing to take away

You cannot shoot your way to a good offense by volume alone. Quality is the requirement; volume is a style choice on top of it.

The clearest proof is in the two corners people overlook. The selective, low-volume teams (Houston, Arizona, Purdue) win roughly nine percentage points more often than the high-volume, low-quality teams (Cal Poly, Elon, Youngstown State), despite taking fewer of the "good" shots. When volume and quality disagree, quality wins, every time. That is why the entire bottom of this chart is locked out of the rankings no matter how much those teams shoot.

This visualization makes that visible in a way a table of correlations cannot: you can watch the green vanish the moment low-quality teams are removed, and you can see for yourself that no amount of horizontal movement (volume) rescues a team that is low on the vertical axis (quality).

What have you done so far?

So far, we've initialized the first version of our core visualization, plotting Shot Volume (x) against Shot Quality (y). Both axes focus on Rim Attempts and 3-Pointers, widely considered the most efficient shots in basketball. Shot Volume is the number of these attempts per game, and Shot Quality is the eFG% on them. We also added a conference filter, allowing the viewer to select only certain conferences (ordered in the dropdown menu by conference rank). You can also click on any individual dot (which are colored by their net rating) to see a more in depth profile on each team's shooting profile (in depth shot quality, shot volume and additional shot diet data). We also have a brush tool allowing you to highlight regions of the chart and will show these same additional stats aggregated for all highlighted teams. Lastly, we're working on building out individual pages for each conference but we haven't set up all conferences or added much content to those that we have created.

What will be the most challenging part to design, and why?

The most challenging aspect of our project will be the temporal aspect we hope to add. We'd like to add shot data for the last 5-10 seasons, to show how shooting trends have changed overall within college basketball, especially within winning teams. Another challenge we expect to face is making the project easily digestible for viewers who may not be familiar with college basketball at all (for example what do all of these statistics and numbers mean). For this we plan to add a large section dedicated to introducing viewers to the concepts we dive more heavily into. We're waiting to add this until we build out more of the project, so we know what stats, terms, and concepts are most important to contextualize.
