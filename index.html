An Interactive Exploration of College Basketball Offenses

By Owen McFadden, Madhav Pudukottai Krishnaraj, Karthik Sankaran, and Yaqub Mir

The analytics revolution gave college coaches a clear formula: attack the rim and shoot threes. Some programs run that play on every single possession, regardless of what the defense gives them. Others only pull the trigger when the opportunity is genuinely earned. The difference is everything.

365 Division I men's teams, two axes. Horizontal is how much they shoot rim attempts and threes—the two most efficient shots in the game. Vertical is how well they shoot them. Dot color is overall team strength: green is elite, red is struggling. Take a moment. What pattern do you see?

Start with the obvious winners. Michigan, Duke, and Florida sit top-right—they shoot a lot of the right shots and they make them. High volume. High quality. The formula working exactly as advertised. More of the right shots equals more wins. Simple.

If running the formula is the answer, every team on the right half should be winning. Look at them. Green, yellow, orange, red—every level of outcome is scattered across the high-volume side. Following the formula is not what separates the good teams from the bad.

Here is why. Youngstown State, Elon, and Cal Poly shoot from rim+3 zones on 81–84% of every possession—they are running the formula on every single play. Defenses know exactly where the ball is going. The result: mediocre conversion rates despite maximum commitment, landing them at NET #219, #222, and #227. It is not that the shots are wrong. It is that a predictable offense stops earning them.

Now the contrast. Purdue takes rim attempts on just 24% of possessions—but converts them at the 100th percentile nationally. Houston gets to the rim on just 20% of shots, at the 99th percentile. Arizona (#3) creates genuine rim opportunities on 40% of possessions, but is conservative with threes, only shooting when open. None of them are chasing the formula. They are waiting for the defense to give it to them.

The best offenses are not the ones that shoot the most rim attempts and threes. They are the ones that only shoot when the defense hands them a genuine opportunity. Volume is a side effect of good offense. Quality is what you have when you stop forcing it.

The one thing to take away

The formula is not wrong. Rim attempts and threes are the most efficient shots in basketball. The mistake is treating them as a quota to hit rather than an opportunity to earn.

Youngstown State shoots from rim+3 zones on 84% of possessions. Arizona, ranked #3 in the country, does it on 67%. Youngstown is telling the defense every single possession what they plan to do. Arizona is creating opportunities and capitalizing on them. The shot types are identical. The approach is completely different. Purdue converts rim attempts at the 100th percentile nationally—not because they force their way there, but because they only go when the defense is out of position. Houston is the same story at the 99th percentile.

This is what the chart makes visible: the teams near the bottom are not there because they chose the wrong shots. They are there because they run a script, and good defenses read scripts. The teams at the top earn each look individually—and when you do that, the volume takes care of itself.
